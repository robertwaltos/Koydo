# Food Science Techniques and Analysis

## Rheology of Food

Rheology studies how food materials deform and flow under applied forces. Understanding rheology is critical for product development and quality control.

### Viscosity

Viscosity measures resistance to flow. Foods can behave as:

- **Newtonian fluids** — viscosity is constant regardless of shear rate (water, simple syrups).
- **Shear-thinning (pseudoplastic)** — viscosity decreases with increasing shear (ketchup, yoghurt). This is why ketchup flows after shaking.
- **Shear-thickening (dilatant)** — viscosity increases with shear (cornstarch–water suspensions).
- **Thixotropic** — viscosity decreases over time at constant shear (some paints, certain condiments).

### Gel Formation

Gels are semi-solid systems where a network of polymer chains traps liquid:

| Gelling Agent | Source | Mechanism | Typical Use |
|--------------|--------|-----------|-------------|
| Gelatin | Animal collagen | Helix formation on cooling | Panna cotta, gummy sweets |
| Pectin | Fruit cell walls | Sugar + acid cross-linking (HM pectin) or calcium bridges (LM pectin) | Jams, jellies |
| Agar | Red seaweed | Hydrogen bonding on cooling | Asian desserts, microbiology |
| Carrageenan | Red seaweed | Helix + cation association | Dairy desserts, plant milks |
| Starch | Grains, tubers | Gelatinisation + retrogradation | Sauces, puddings |

## Water Activity (a_w)

Water activity is the ratio of the vapour pressure of water in a food to the vapour pressure of pure water at the same temperature. It determines:

- **Microbial growth** — most bacteria need a_w > 0.91; moulds can grow at a_w ≥ 0.65.
- **Chemical reactions** — lipid oxidation accelerates at very low a_w (< 0.3) and intermediate a_w.
- **Texture** — crispy foods require a_w < 0.4; soft baked goods target a_w 0.6–0.9.

## Caramelisation

Caramelisation is the pyrolysis of sugars at high temperature (above 160 °C for sucrose) in the absence of amino acids (distinguishing it from the Maillard reaction).

### Process

1. Melting of the crystalline sugar.
2. Isomerisation and dehydration — loss of water molecules.
3. Condensation and polymerisation — formation of caramel colours (caramelans, caramelens, caramelins).
4. Fragmentation — production of volatile aroma compounds (diacetyl, hydroxymethylfurfural, maltol).

### Controlling Caramelisation

- **Dry method** — sugar heated alone; requires careful attention to prevent burning.
- **Wet method** — sugar dissolved in water first; more forgiving but slower.
- Adding a small amount of acid (lemon juice) or glucose syrup prevents recrystallisation during cooling.

## Enzymatic Browning

Enzymatic browning occurs when polyphenol oxidase (PPO) catalyses the oxidation of phenolic compounds to quinones, which polymerise into brown melanin pigments. This is the reaction behind browning of cut apples, avocados, and bananas.

### Prevention

- **Acidification** — lemon juice lowers pH below PPO's optimal range.
- **Blanching** — brief heat treatment denatures PPO.
- **Reducing agents** — ascorbic acid (vitamin C) reduces quinones back to colourless phenols.
- **Exclusion of oxygen** — vacuum packing or submerging in water.

## Leavening in Baking

### Biological Leavening

Yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae) ferments sugars to CO₂ and ethanol. The CO₂ is trapped in the gluten network, causing dough to rise. Optimal fermentation temperature: 25–30 °C.

### Chemical Leavening

- **Baking soda** (NaHCO₃) — requires an acid (buttermilk, vinegar, cream of tartar) to release CO₂.
- **Baking powder** — contains both a base and an acid salt; "double-acting" varieties release CO₂ once when moistened and again when heated.

### Mechanical Leavening

Whipping incorporates air into batters (e.g., sponge cakes). Egg-white foams can increase volume 6–8 times.

### Steam Leavening

High-moisture doughs (choux pastry, popovers) rely on water converting to steam (1 700× volume expansion) during baking.

## Sensory Evaluation

Sensory evaluation applies scientific methods to measure human perception of food attributes.

### Test Types

| Test | Purpose | Format |
|------|---------|--------|
| Triangle test | Detect overall difference between two samples | Three coded samples; identify the odd one |
| Duo-trio | Compare sample to a reference | Three samples; match to reference |
| Ranking | Order samples by intensity of an attribute | Rank from least to most |
| Descriptive analysis | Profile all detectable attributes | Trained panel scores on intensity scales |
| Hedonic scale | Measure consumer acceptance | 9-point like–dislike scale |

### Minimising Bias

- Random three-digit codes for sample labels.
- Balanced presentation order (counterbalanced design).
- Rinsing palate between samples.
- Controlled lighting (red lights mask colour differences when testing flavour alone).

## Food Additives

### Categories

| Category | Function | Examples |
|----------|----------|----------|
| Preservatives | Inhibit microbial growth | Sodium benzoate (E211), potassium sorbate (E202) |
| Antioxidants | Delay lipid oxidation | BHA (E320), BHT (E321), ascorbic acid (E300) |
| Emulsifiers | Stabilise emulsions | Lecithin (E322), mono- and diglycerides (E471) |
| Thickeners | Increase viscosity | Xanthan gum (E415), guar gum (E412) |
| Colourants | Standardise appearance | Caramel colour (E150), paprika extract (E160c) |
| Sweeteners | Replace or supplement sugar | Sucralose (E955), stevia glycosides (E960) |

### Regulatory Context

Food additives must undergo safety evaluation before approval. The Codex Alimentarius (FAO/WHO) sets international standards. Regional authorities (FDA in the US, EFSA in the EU) set Acceptable Daily Intake (ADI) values.

## Thermal Processing Calculations

### D-value

The decimal reduction time (D-value) is the time at a specific temperature required to reduce the microbial population by 90 % (one log cycle). For example, D₁₂₁ for Clostridium botulinum spores is approximately 0.21 minutes.

### z-value

The z-value is the temperature increase required to reduce the D-value by one log cycle. For C. botulinum: z ≈ 10 °C.

### 12-D Process (Botulinum Cook)

Commercial canning applies a 12-D process — enough heat to reduce C. botulinum spores by 12 log cycles, ensuring a probability of survival of less than 10⁻¹² per container.
