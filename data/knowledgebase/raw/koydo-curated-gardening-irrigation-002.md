# Irrigation Systems and Plant Care

## Irrigation Methods

### Drip Irrigation

Drip systems deliver water at low pressure through emitters placed near each plant's root zone. Advantages include:

- 30–50 % less water use compared to overhead sprinklers.
- Reduced leaf wetness — lowers risk of foliar diseases.
- Uniform delivery — each emitter provides a measured flow rate (typically 1–4 litres per hour).

A basic drip system consists of a pressure regulator, backflow preventer, filter, main supply tubing (12–16 mm polyethylene), and emitter lines or point-source drippers.

### Soaker Hoses

Porous hoses seep water along their entire length, ideal for row crops and hedgerows. They are cheaper than drip emitters but less precise.

### Overhead Sprinklers

Best suited for lawns and large-area coverage. Oscillating, impact, and rotary types exist. Water loss to wind and evaporation can be significant.

### Sub-irrigation (Wicking Beds)

Water is held in a reservoir below the growing medium and moves upward via capillary action. Self-watering containers and wicking beds reduce watering frequency to once or twice per week.

## Understanding Plant Water Needs

Plants lose water through transpiration—evaporation from stomata on leaf surfaces. Transpiration rate depends on:

- **Temperature** — higher temperatures increase transpiration.
- **Humidity** — low humidity pulls moisture from leaves faster.
- **Wind** — increases the evaporation gradient.
- **Leaf area** — larger canopy = more transpiration.

### Wilting Point and Field Capacity

- **Field capacity** — the amount of water soil holds after gravitational drainage.
- **Wilting point** — the moisture level below which roots can no longer extract water.
- Optimal soil moisture for most crops lies between 50–80 % of field capacity.

## Pruning Fundamentals

Pruning removes unwanted or damaged plant tissue to improve health, shape, and productivity.

### Types of Pruning Cuts

1. **Heading cut** — shortens a branch to an outward-facing bud, encouraging bushier growth.
2. **Thinning cut** — removes an entire branch at its point of origin, opening the canopy for air and light.
3. **Deadheading** — removing spent flowers to redirect energy toward new blooms or fruit.

### When to Prune

| Plant Type | Best Time | Reason |
|------------|-----------|--------|
| Spring-flowering shrubs | After bloom | Flower on old wood |
| Summer-flowering shrubs | Late winter | Flower on new wood |
| Fruit trees | Late dormancy (late winter) | Minimise disease entry |
| Roses | Late winter / early spring | Stimulate strong new canes |

## Pest Identification and Management

### Common Garden Pests

- **Aphids** — soft-bodied insects clustering on new growth; controlled by ladybirds, lacewings, or insecticidal soap.
- **Slugs and snails** — feed at night on foliage; deterred by copper tape, beer traps, or iron phosphate pellets.
- **Caterpillars** — butterfly and moth larvae chewing leaves; BT (Bacillus thuringiensis) spray is a targeted biological control.
- **Whiteflies** — tiny white flying insects on leaf undersides; yellow sticky traps and neem oil are effective.

### Beneficial Insects

- **Ladybirds** — consume up to 5 000 aphids in a lifetime.
- **Hoverflies** — larvae eat aphids; adults pollinate flowers.
- **Ground beetles** — nocturnal predators of slugs and caterpillars.
- **Parasitic wasps** — lay eggs in pest larvae, naturally controlling populations.

## Seed Starting

### Indoor Starting

1. Fill seed trays with sterile seed-starting mix.
2. Sow seeds at the depth recommended on the packet (general rule: 2× seed diameter).
3. Maintain 18–24 °C and consistent moisture.
4. Provide 14–16 hours of light daily (grow lights supplement natural light).
5. Harden off seedlings over 7–10 days before transplanting outdoors.

### Direct Sowing

Some crops (beans, peas, carrots, radishes) prefer direct sowing because they dislike root disturbance. Sow after the last frost date for warm-season crops, or 4–6 weeks before the first frost for cool-season crops in autumn.

## Mulching in Depth

| Mulch Material | Depth | Notes |
|---------------|-------|-------|
| Straw | 10–15 cm | Excellent for vegetable beds; decomposes slowly |
| Wood chips | 7–10 cm | Best around trees and shrubs; avoid touching trunks |
| Leaf mould | 5–7 cm | Rich in fungi; ideal for woodland plantings |
| Cardboard | 1 layer | Weed barrier under other mulches |
| Living mulch (clover) | N/A | Fixes nitrogen; mow periodically |

## Succession Planting

Planting the same crop at 2–3 week intervals extends the harvest window. For example, sowing lettuce every two weeks from early spring through early autumn provides continuous salad greens rather than a single glut.
