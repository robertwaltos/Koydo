# Foundations of Food Science

## What Is Food Science?

Food science is the interdisciplinary study of the physical, chemical, and biological properties of food ingredients and the principles underlying food processing, preservation, and safety. It draws from chemistry, biochemistry, microbiology, engineering, and nutrition.

## Macronutrient Chemistry

### Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are polyhydroxy aldehydes or ketones, or compounds that yield them on hydrolysis.

- **Monosaccharides** — simple sugars (glucose, fructose, galactose).
- **Disaccharides** — two monosaccharides linked by a glycosidic bond (sucrose = glucose + fructose; lactose = glucose + galactose).
- **Polysaccharides** — long chains: starch (energy storage in plants), glycogen (energy storage in animals), cellulose (structural fibre).

#### Starch Gelatinisation

When starch granules are heated in water above their gelatinisation temperature (60–80 °C depending on source), they absorb water, swell, and the amylose leaches out, thickening the liquid. Cooling the gel causes retrogradation — amylose chains reassociate into a more ordered structure, which is why bread goes stale.

### Proteins

Proteins are polymers of amino acids linked by peptide bonds. In food they serve structural, enzymatic, and functional roles.

#### Denaturation

Heat, acid, mechanical action, or salt can unfold (denature) protein tertiary structure:

| Trigger | Example |
|---------|---------|
| Heat | Egg white solidifies at ~62 °C |
| Acid | Milk curdles when lemon juice is added (casein precipitation) |
| Mechanical | Whipping egg whites traps air in denatured protein films |
| Salt | Salting fish draws water out and alters protein conformation |

#### Gluten

Gluten is a network of glutenin (elasticity) and gliadin (extensibility) proteins formed when wheat flour is hydrated and mixed. Gluten development is essential for bread structure but undesirable in tender pastries.

### Lipids (Fats and Oils)

Lipids are hydrophobic molecules — triglycerides (three fatty acids esterified to glycerol) are the most common in food.

- **Saturated fatty acids** — no double bonds; solid at room temperature (butter, coconut oil).
- **Monounsaturated** — one double bond (olive oil, avocado oil).
- **Polyunsaturated** — two or more double bonds (sunflower oil, fish oil).

#### Smoke Point

The temperature at which an oil begins to break down and produce visible smoke. Choosing an oil with an appropriate smoke point prevents off-flavours and the formation of harmful decomposition products.

## The Maillard Reaction

The Maillard reaction is a non-enzymatic browning reaction between reducing sugars and amino acids occurring at temperatures above ~140 °C. It produces hundreds of flavour and aroma compounds, and the characteristic brown colour of seared meat, toasted bread, and roasted coffee.

### Stages

1. **Condensation** — a reducing sugar reacts with an amino group to form a glycosylamine.
2. **Amadori rearrangement** — the glycosylamine rearranges to a ketoamine.
3. **Advanced reactions** — the Amadori compound undergoes dehydration, fragmentation, and condensation to produce melanoidins (brown pigments) and volatile aroma compounds (furanones, pyrazines, thiophenes).

### Factors Affecting the Maillard Reaction

- **Temperature** — faster at higher temperatures.
- **pH** — favoured in alkaline conditions (hence pretzel dipping in lye).
- **Water activity** — optimal at intermediate moisture (0.4–0.8 a_w).
- **Sugar type** — pentoses (ribose) react faster than hexoses (glucose).

## Emulsions

An emulsion is a stable mixture of two immiscible liquids — typically oil and water — held together by an emulsifier.

### Types

- **Oil-in-water (O/W)** — oil droplets dispersed in water (milk, mayonnaise, vinaigrettes).
- **Water-in-oil (W/O)** — water droplets dispersed in oil (butter, margarine).

### Emulsifiers

Emulsifiers are amphiphilic molecules with both hydrophilic and hydrophobic regions:

- **Lecithin** (egg yolk) — a phospholipid.
- **Casein** (milk protein) — adsorbs at the oil-water interface.
- **Mono- and di-glycerides** — common food-industry emulsifiers.
- **Mustard** — contains mucilage that stabilises vinaigrettes.

### Breaking an Emulsion

Emulsions can destabilise through:
- **Creaming** — density-driven separation (lighter phase rises).
- **Coalescence** — droplets merge into larger drops.
- **Flocculation** — droplets clump without merging.

## Fermentation

Fermentation is the metabolic conversion of sugars to acids, gases, or alcohol by microorganisms.

| Fermentation Type | Organism | Primary Product | Food Examples |
|-------------------|----------|-----------------|---------------|
| Alcoholic | Saccharomyces cerevisiae | Ethanol + CO₂ | Bread, beer, wine |
| Lactic acid | Lactobacillus spp. | Lactic acid | Yoghurt, kimchi, sauerkraut |
| Acetic acid | Acetobacter spp. | Acetic acid | Vinegar |
| Propionic acid | Propionibacterium spp. | Propionic acid + CO₂ | Swiss cheese (eye formation) |

### Benefits of Fermentation

- Preserves food by lowering pH or producing alcohol.
- Enhances flavour complexity.
- Increases bioavailability of nutrients (e.g., phytate reduction in sourdough).
- Produces probiotics that may support gut health.

## Food Preservation Methods

| Method | Principle | Shelf Life Extension |
|--------|-----------|---------------------|
| Refrigeration (0–4 °C) | Slows microbial growth | Days to weeks |
| Freezing (−18 °C or lower) | Halts microbial activity | Months to years |
| Canning | Heat sterilisation + hermetic seal | Years |
| Dehydration | Removes water; reduces a_w below 0.6 | Months to years |
| Salting / Sugaring | Lowers a_w via osmotic pressure | Weeks to months |
| Smoking | Surface drying + antimicrobial phenols | Weeks |
| Pickling | Acidification (pH < 4.6) | Months |
| Irradiation | Ionising radiation damages microbial DNA | Variable |

## Food Safety Fundamentals

### The Danger Zone

Bacteria multiply most rapidly between 5 °C and 60 °C. Food should not remain in this temperature range for more than 2 hours (1 hour if ambient temperature exceeds 32 °C).

### Critical Control Points

Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points (HACCP) is a systematic approach to food safety:

1. Conduct a hazard analysis.
2. Identify critical control points (CCPs).
3. Establish critical limits for each CCP.
4. Monitor CCPs.
5. Establish corrective actions.
6. Verify the system.
7. Keep records.
